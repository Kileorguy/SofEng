The Barong, a symbol of righteousness in Balinese folklore, is often depicted as a lion, cow, or dragon. Its sacred head, carved from pule tree wood and blessed by a priest, is crafted into a mask kept in temples. Joined with its body for traditional dances, the Barong represents Virtue, acting as a guardian angel to protect people and maintain the balance between good and evil.
Opposing Good, Rangda, the Queen of Demons, represents evil in Balinese folklore. Leading an army of witches, she battles the Barong in an eternal struggle. In the Barong Dance, this conflict is depicted as a reflection of life's duality. Although Rangda cured the queen, she used dark magic to seize control of the kingdom, spreading chaos and suffering across Saha.
The Barong dance, one of Bali's most sacred, was once reserved for special ceremonies but is now often performed daily for tourists. It requires two performers: one for the head and one for the body. The dance narrates the story of Barong battling the evil witch Rangda. Under Rangda's spell, dancers fall into a trance and stab themselves, but Barong's protective magic prevents injury. After the trance, Barong and his army defeat Rangda, who reincarnates, symbolizing the ongoing struggle between light and darkness. In the Calon Arang drama, Barong counters Rangda's magic, and tranced men stab themselves with kris daggers but are revived by holy water, restoring balance.
